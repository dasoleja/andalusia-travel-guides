Granada Cathedral & Royal Chapel

Built on the Site of the Last Islamic Kingdom

At a Glance

Essential Details

  • Location: Gran Vía de Colón 5, Granada 18010 (in city center)
  • Built: 1518-1664 CE (Christian cathedral built after 1492 conquest)
  • Entry: €5-6 (combo with Royal Chapel, Alhambra Card covers both)
  • Hours: 10:00 AM - 1:30 PM, 4:00 PM - 7:00 PM (summer)
  • Estimated Time: 1-1.5 hours
  • Best Time: Late morning (10:00-11:30 AM) before peak heat and crowds
Included in Your Granada Card: Both Cathedral and Royal Chapel are covered by your Granada Card purchase. No additional tickets needed.
What You'll See

The Cathedral (Interior)

Built as the Christian answer to the Alhambra and Islamic Granada. It's vast, soaring, and ornate—the opposite of Islamic geometric simplicity. Heavy baroque architecture with gilded altars, intricate carvings, and imposing size meant to express Christian power and permanence.

The Royal Chapel (Capilla Real)

The most important section for understanding Spanish history. This chapel contains the tombs of the Catholic Monarchs—Ferdinand and Isabella—who conquered Granada in 1492 and unified Spain. Below them lie their descendants, including Philip the Mad and Juana the Mad. This is where you see the symbolic "end" of the Reconquista.

Historical Significance

The Cathedral was built on the site of a mosque and incorporates some of the mosque's original structure beneath Christian additions. Standing in this building, you literally stand at the intersection of Islamic and Christian Spain. The tombs of the monarchs who ended 800 years of Islamic rule are directly in front of you.

The soaring baroque interior of Granada Cathedral

Historical Context

1492: The Year Everything Changed

In January 1492, the Catholic Monarchs (Ferdinand and Isabella) captured Granada, ending 800 years of Islamic rule in Iberia. The last Nasrid ruler, Boabdil, was forced to surrender and eventually left Spain for North Africa. Legend says he wept as he looked back at the Alhambra, saying: "How sad it is to be like a woman in a time of war" (blaming himself for losing the kingdom).

The Cathedral: A Monument to Victory

The Catholic Monarchs immediately began constructing this cathedral as a symbol of Christian triumph. It took 146 years to complete (1518-1664). The Royal Chapel, built first, was meant to be their burial site—a permanent marking of their victory and unification of Spain.

The Tombs You'll See

Ferdinand & Isabella: The monarchs who conquered Granada. Isabella was also the patron of Columbus, whose voyage in 1492 (the same year as the conquest) marked Spain's rise as a world power.

Juana the Mad & Philip the Handsome: Their daughter and son-in-law, also buried here. Philip died young, and Juana never recovered—she allegedly traveled the countryside with his corpse for years, earning her nickname.

Connection to Your Trip: Visit this after the Alhambra (August 1). See the Islamic palace first, then come here to see the Christian response. It's a powerful contrast—beauty vs. power, Islamic geometric design vs. baroque ornament, the old world vs. the new.
Practical Information

Stroller & Accessibility

  • Strollers: Good! Wide aisles, flat floors in main cathedral and chapel areas
  • Stairs: Some areas have steps, but the main route is stroller-friendly
  • Crowds: Usually quieter than Alhambra, especially in early morning

Dress Code

  • Required: Shoulders and knees covered (it's a working Catholic cathedral)
  • Note: Stricter than the mosque—head coverings for women recommended but not required

What's Included with Granada Card

  • Cathedral entrance
  • Royal Chapel entrance
  • No additional cost beyond your card

Photography

Photography generally allowed in the Cathedral and Royal Chapel. The tombs are particularly photogenic if you're interested in history.

Heat Management (August 1)

Your Timing

If you visit the Cathedral/Chapel in the morning before the Alhambra (or right after), you'll be in the thick interior—which is cool and air-conditioned. The Cathedral is less physically demanding than the Alhambra, so good for a mid-heat break.

Suggestion: Visit Cathedral/Chapel first (10:00-11:30 AM), cool off inside, then head to Alhambra at 1:30 PM entrance. The indoor time will help manage the afternoon heat.

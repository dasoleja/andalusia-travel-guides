Giralda Tower & Seville Cathedral

Spain's Iconic Minaret-Turned-Bell-Tower

At a Glance

Essential Details

  • Location: Plaza Virgen de los Reyes, Seville 41004
  • Built: Minaret: 1198 (Almohad), Bell Tower: 1565-1568 (Christian addition)
  • Height: 97.5 meters (320 feet)—Spain's tallest tower when built
  • Entry: €5 (cathedral entrance)
  • Hours: 11:00 AM - 5:00 PM (summer schedule varies)
  • Estimated Time: 1.5 hours (includes cathedral + climb)
  • Best Time: Early morning (11:00 AM opening) or late afternoon (3:00-4:00 PM)
Iconic Symbol: The Giralda is one of Spain's most recognizable monuments, visible from all over Seville. It's on the city's coat of arms and is the symbol of Andalusian heritage.
What You'll See

The Giralda Tower (The Minaret)

Originally built as the minaret (call-to-prayer tower) for the Almohad Mosque in the 12th century. It's pure Islamic geometric beauty—a square tower covered in intricate arabesque patterns (carved stucco lattice). The base is the original Islamic structure; the top was added by Christians.

The Climb (34 Ramps, No Stairs!)

The tower has 34 ramps instead of stairs—a unique feature. Originally, the imam would ride a horse up the ramps to the top to call the faithful to prayer. Modern visitors climb (no horse needed). The ramps are manageable, and the view from the top is spectacular: all of Seville spread below.

The Bell Section (Top)

The top 16 meters were added by Christians in the 16th century. It's a bell tower with a weathervane (veleta) shaped like a woman—the "Giraldilla"—which spins in the wind. It's become Seville's symbol. The contrast between the Islamic base and Christian top is striking.

The Cathedral

One of Europe's largest cathedrals, built on the site of the Almohad Mosque. The cathedral is vast and baroque—ornate altars, golden decorations, and immense scale meant to showcase Christian power. Inside, you'll find the tomb of Christopher Columbus.

The iconic Giralda Tower with the Cathedral facade, showing both Islamic and Christian architecture

Historical Context

The Tower's Evolution

1198 (Almohad Era): Built as a minaret for the great mosque. It's one of the finest examples of Almohad architecture—bold geometric patterns and perfectly proportioned.

1402 (Christian Era): The mosque is converted to a cathedral. Christians keep the minaret (it's too magnificent to destroy) but add a bell tower on top.

1565-1568: The current bell tower and weathervane are added, creating the hybrid structure we see today.

Seville's Rise to Power

After 1492, Seville became Spain's wealthiest city—the center of the Spanish colonial empire. All gold and treasures from the Americas passed through Seville's harbor. The cathedral and Giralda were rebuilt and expanded to reflect this wealth and power. Columbus's tomb is here because Seville was the embarkation point for his voyages.

What You're Seeing: The Giralda represents the transition from Islamic to Christian Spain. The base is 12th-century Islamic perfection; the top is 16th-century Christian Renaissance. Stand at the base and you can see exactly where one era ends and the other begins.
Practical Information

The Climb: Important Details

  • Ramps vs. Stairs: 34 gently sloped ramps, much easier than stairs
  • Duration: 10-15 minutes to climb up, same to descend
  • Fitness Level: Moderate fitness fine; it's not strenuous
  • Narrow Passages: Some sections are tight, especially where ramps curve
  • Children: Ages 6+ can usually do it; younger kids may struggle
  • View at Top: Worth the climb; 360° views of Seville
Heat Note: The climb is partially shaded (through the tower), but the top section is exposed. You'll warm up during the climb. Do it early in the day.

Stroller & Accessibility

  • Strollers: NOT suitable for the climb. Leave them at the entrance or with someone outside.
  • Cathedral Floor: Flat and stroller-friendly
  • Climb: Ramps are navigable but narrow; might be tricky with small children on shoulders
Tip: One adult climbs the tower (15 min), while the other stays with kids below in the cathedral. Rotate if desired. The cathedral is worth an hour on its own.

Dress Code & Etiquette

  • Required: Shoulders and knees covered (it's a functioning cathedral)
  • Respectful Behavior: Quiet voices; it's a sacred space
Heat Management (July 28-31)

Your Timing

You're visiting during peak summer heat. The cathedral interior is cool (thick stone walls), but the tower climb has exposed sections.

Strategy: Arrive right at opening (11:00 AM) or go in late afternoon (3:30-4:00 PM). Climb the tower first (quick, while you have energy), then explore the cathedral's cool interior for 45 minutes.

What to Bring

  • Water (you'll sweat during the climb)
  • High-SPF sunscreen
  • Hat or cap
  • Light clothing

Alcázar Palace (Royal Palace)

Europe's Oldest Royal Palace Still in Use

At a Glance

Essential Details

  • Location: Patio de Banderas, Seville 41004 (next to the Cathedral)
  • Built: 14th century onwards (Almohad fortress, expanded continuously through 19th century)
  • Entry: €10-12 (depends on special exhibitions)
  • Hours: 9:30 AM - 5:00 PM (summer schedule extends to 7:00 PM)
  • Estimated Time: 2 hours minimum (palace + gardens)
  • Best Time: Early morning (opens at 9:30 AM) to avoid crowds and peak heat
Still in Use: The Alcázar is the oldest royal palace in Europe that's still used by the Spanish royal family for state functions. Not a museum frozen in time—it's a living palace.
What You'll See

The Palace Complex

Unlike the Alhambra (one concentrated palace), the Alcázar is a sprawling complex that grew over centuries. Islamic sections blend with Christian additions. You'll walk through courtyards, throne rooms, and private chambers, each with its own architectural style showing different eras.

Key Rooms to See

  • Palacio del Gotjco: Gothic palace added by Christians
  • Patio de las Doncellas (Court of the Maidens): A beautiful Almohad courtyard with central pool and arcaded porticos
  • Throne Rooms: Ornate medieval chambers with arabesque decorations
  • Royal Chambers: Private apartments with mudejar (Islamic) and Christian designs

The Gardens

Expansive, terraced gardens with trees, fountains, and cool water channels. The gardens are designed to cool the air and provide respite from heat. Multiple layers: the Plaza de Armas (outer courtyard), tree-lined paths, fountain gardens. This is your heat refuge.

An ornate courtyard within the Alcázar, showing the blend of Moorish and Christian architecture

Historical Context

From Fortress to Palace

11th-14th centuries (Islamic): Built as a fortress by successive Islamic rulers, especially during the Almohad period. Defensive walls, courtyards designed for security and cooling.

1492 onwards (Christian): After conquest, the Catholic Monarchs converted it to a royal residence. Rather than destroy, they adapted—adding Christian rooms while keeping Islamic courtyards and gardens.

15th-19th centuries: Continuous expansion and modification. Different monarchs left their mark. Renaissance additions, baroque flourishes, neoclassical sections.

Why It Survived & Thrived

Unlike the Alhambra, which was conquered and then abandoned, the Alcázar was continuously used. Spanish kings needed a palace in Seville (the wealthy colonial capital), so the Alcázar was maintained, expanded, and improved. This continuous use is why it remains in excellent condition.

What You're Seeing: A real, working royal palace—not a museum. The blend of styles shows how Islamic and Christian cultures coexisted and merged over centuries in Spain. It's less "frozen in time" and more "living history."
Practical Information

Layout & Navigation

  • Size: Large complex; easy to get lost without a map (provided at entrance)
  • Route: Usually one-way flow through palace rooms, then gardens
  • Duration: 90 minutes to 2 hours depending on pace
  • Audio Guide: Available (small fee), helpful for understanding the different sections

Stroller & Accessibility

  • Palace Interior: Some stairs, tight passages; strollers tricky but doable on main routes
  • Gardens: Flat, tree-shaded paths; stroller-friendly
  • Recommendation: Use strollers for gardens; carry young children through narrow palace sections or use baby carriers
Tip: The gardens are the best part for kids—room to move, shade, water features, and beauty. Plan 30-45 minutes for gardens even if you rush through the palace.

Water & Facilities

  • Fountains: Multiple throughout gardens; water drinkable
  • Bathrooms: Available in palace section
  • Shade: Abundant in gardens (orange trees, date palms, fig trees)
  • Seating: Benches throughout gardens
Heat Management (July 28-31)
Advantage: The Alcázar was designed to handle heat. The gardens are naturally cooled by fountains and trees. You'll actually feel cooler here than in many Seville streets.

Strategy

  • Arrive Early: Open at 9:30 AM. Arrive by 9:45 AM to beat crowds and heat.
  • Pace: Don't rush. Walk slowly through palace rooms (they're cooler inside). Spend 30-45 min on palace, then move to gardens for the remaining time.
  • Garden Rest: Find a shaded bench by a fountain and sit for 15 minutes. This is your heat break.

What to Bring

  • Water (fountain fills help, but bring your own)
  • Hats
  • Sunscreen
  • Light, loose clothing
